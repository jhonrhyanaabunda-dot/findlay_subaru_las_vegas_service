02The intervals

What desert heat actually changes.

Subaru's maintenance schedule was written for a temperate average. Las Vegas isn't temperate average.

A few intervals worth knowing, what the manual says, and what the valley actually does to your Subaru.

12,000 miAir

Cabin air filter

The valley cuts that interval in half. Between construction dust and dry lake-bed wind, we've cut open filters at 7,000 miles that look two years old. If your AC airflow has dropped, it's time.

Seal lifeClimate

AC system

Not the refrigerant, the seals. They dry out faster when the AC runs at maximum capacity four months straight. Ice-cold last summer and merely cool this summer? Probably the seals.

137,500 miCoolant

Super Coolant

We don't sell early flushes. Subaru Super Coolant is rated 11 years or 137,500 miles to first replacement, then 6 years or 75,000 after. We follow that spec. If yours doesn't need one, we say so.

30,000 miBrakes

Brake fluid

Tested with a meter, not a calendar. Every 30,000 miles per Subaru's manual. The Vegas heat-cool cycle pulls moisture in faster than the Pacific Northwest spec assumed. If dry, we tell you.

30,000 miTrans

CVT fluid

Severe-duty matters. Subaru's interval is conservative; a 30,000-mile inspection covers most owners through warranty. Tow Charleston, drive Mojave, commute I-15 in summer? Severe duty.

OEM filterEngine

Engine oil

The right filter for your engine. 4.4 quarts of 0W-20 on the 2.5L NA, 4.8 on the 2.4T turbo. Wrong filter has the wrong bypass valve, and that's how turbos get oil-starved at 80,000 miles.

A quick-lube oil change in the valley typically runs $80–$140. On a non-turbo Subaru, the filter is a minor difference. On a turbo Outback, Ascent, or WRX, the wrong filter has the wrong bypass valve.

04The bench

Factory-trained Subaru technicians, not generalists.

A Subaru is not a Toyota with a different badge.

The Boxer engine has horizontally opposed cylinders that turn an oil change into something more involved than tipping the pan over a bucket. Most general-purpose shops can do an oil change on a Subaru. The CVT fluid problem

This one is worth slowing down for, because it's the most expensive avoidable mistake we see.

Subaru CVT fluid is not one fluid. It's four:

  • CVTF-II, older Lineartronic non-turbo. The fluid most parts catalogs default to.
  • CVTF-III,2020 and newer Legacy and Outback 2.5L non-turbo. Different additive package, not interchangeable.
  • High Torque CVTF, WRX and older H-6 Outbacks (the TR690 in turbo form).
  • CVTF LV, Ascent and 2020+ Outback 2.4 turbo. Low-viscosity, very specific fill procedure.

A general parts store doesn't always know which fluid your VIN takes. If your Outback ends up with CVTF-II in a transmission spec'd for CVTF-III, you don't notice immediately. You notice 30,000 miles later, when the chain starts slipping and the diagnosis is a $5,800 transmission. We carry every fluid Subaru calls for, and we look up your VIN before we open a bottle.

Symmetrical AWD and tires

The center differential continuously distributes torque to all four wheels and expects all four wheels to be turning at the same circumference. Subaru's tolerance is 1/4 inch of circumference difference, max, roughly 2/32-inch tread depth across the four tires.

Mount one new tire to a set of three half-worn ones and you've created a slip the differential can't ignore. The differential doesn't fail dramatically; it gets warm, then warmer, then one day it doesn't.

We measure tread depth on all four tires before any single-tire replacement and recommend tire shaving when it'll save you a $1,400 set of tires.

08The questions owners ask

Frequently asked questions about Subaru service in Las Vegas.

How much does a Subaru oil change cost in Las Vegas?

RepairPal's full-synthetic oil change estimate runs $142–$170 nationally, the universal Subaru figure across all four major model estimator pages.

Our current posted price is on service specials, or call 702-495-2149 for a quote on your VIN. Every oil change here includes a Genuine Subaru oil filter, the right oil weight for your engine (4.4 quarts of 0W-20 on the 2.5L NA, 4.8 quarts on the 2.4T turbo), tire pressure adjustment, and a free multi-point inspection.

A quick-lube oil change in the valley typically runs $80–$140 for synthetic, but the filter is rarely the OEM Subaru filter. On a turbo Outback, Ascent, or WRX, the wrong filter has a wrong bypass valve, and a wrong bypass valve under turbo oil pressure is how turbos get oil-starved at 80,000 miles.

How long does Express Service take?

60 to 75 minutes for an oil change with tire rotation. Walk in any time during service hours: Monday–Friday 7 AM to 6 PM, Saturday 7 AM to 5 PM.

Mornings before 10 AM and the Tuesday–Thursday window are the shortest waits.

Does Subaru of Las Vegas have loaner cars?

Loaners are available for qualifying longer appointments, brake work, EyeSight recalibration, recall repairs, and major service visits. The fleet is finite and worth booking ahead.

Mention loaner availability when you call to schedule, and the Service BDC team will confirm what's available for your appointment date.

What are your service hours?

Monday–Friday 7 AM to 6 PM, Saturday 7 AM to 5 PM. Closed Sunday.

Online scheduling at subaruoflasvegas.com/service/schedule-service is open 24/7. For same-day issues, call 702-495-2149.

Can I service my Subaru at any retailer, or does it have to come back to you?

Any authorized Subaru retailer can perform warranty and routine service. The warranty travels with the car, not the dealer.

That said, if you keep your records here we'll know your Subaru, what's been done, what's coming up, what you've asked us to keep an eye on. Anthony, Romeo, Yasir, Shawn, and Carmine are all reachable; we're a small enough team that you'll see the same faces twice.

Why service at the dealer instead of a quick-lube?

Three answers, in order of how often they cost real money.

The CVT fluid issue. Subaru runs four different CVT fluids and they aren't interchangeable. A non-Subaru shop putting CVTF-II in a 2026 Outback that takes CVTF-III is the kind of mistake that surfaces 30,000 miles later as a $5,800 transmission.

The EyeSight recalibration issue. After a windshield replacement, even at a great glass shop, your stereo cameras need to agree on what they're seeing. We have the targets, the lift, and the factory procedure on site. A shop without that equipment can replace the glass beautifully and still leave you with EyeSight permanently disabled.

The records issue. If a powertrain claim ever happens, the conversation goes faster when the service history shows the right oil weight, the right filter, and a multi-point inspection at every interval.
